    I used to distinguish bots and agents purely based on their capabilities, whether they can reason, call tools, or finish complex tasks.
    
    But lately, I’ve realized the access point itself shapes how users perceive them entirely.
    
    If an agent can only be activated via commands, dedicated channels or temporary popups, it feels like just a clickable feature to users. You summon it when needed, then dismiss it once you’re done.
    
    Now imagine if your agent was a regular contact instead.
    Contacts hold a permanent spot in your space, build ongoing relationships, and let you revisit conversations anytime. You don’t have to spin everything up from scratch each time; you simply pick back up an existing dialogue.
    
    That’s the core idea I built ClawChat around: agents don’t have to function like throwaway bots. They deserve a stable social interface. They stay clearly identifiable as AI agents, yet you can add them, look them up, and drop them straight into group chats just like human contacts.
    
    I don’t think every single agent needs this format. Some are better off staying simple tool shortcuts. But for long-running agents that rely on persistent context and team collaboration, the contact-based model delivers a far more natural experience.
