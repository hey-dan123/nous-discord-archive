    I've been trying to roll out agents across businesses and I think the findings are pretty interesting. Instead of having employees manage the agents or "AI-enable" the workforce, I did the opposite. 
    I let AI be the one that manages my employees for me. For the real estate business, I have a caller that calls a hundred leads a day to acquire properties of people that may be interested in selling, her day looks as follows: An AI dispatches leads to her through telgram, she responds to the message card which contains the lead and the LLM automatically logs that data in my DB. Once logged, a realtor is assigned to that specific listing, and here is where hermes does its best work: It logs the realtor feedback the same way that it logs the caller feedback and can actually take action. Some examples include: If the realtor wants to set an appointment, hermes automatically sets a cron and pings them. If the realtor doesn't respond to the lead in 12 hours, they are contacted privately. If they don't respond in 24 hours, their manager is notified. All dashboards for this business are remarkably simple, all of them just use streamlit, and almost all of the data entry is done through hermes. Their purpose is to showcase the work, progress, financials, and for legal purposes. 
    As for F&B and Service Businesses, it is a generic ordering bot. It takes the order of the user, is knowledgeable on the menu/services, files support tickets for things like: "My order came in wrong and others..." but one thing about these bots is that they are great at upselling. I wouldn't feel honest if I would tell you a specific number like: Hermes increased my average order price by 20%, but what I can say is users tend to order the thing that the agent is trying to upsell them on. 
    I am open to any questions, for a more detailed breakdown of the financial structure, the business decisions, the agent archeticture, I'm happy to connect.
